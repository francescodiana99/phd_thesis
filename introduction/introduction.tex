\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Context of the Thesis}
In the last years, the increasing availability of connected devices has progressively shifted the data generation process from centralized infrastructures towards large populations of edge devices, including smartphones, wearables, vehicles and sensors.
According to~\citeA{cisco2023annual}, the number of networked devices and connections was expected to reach 29.3 billion by 2023, corresponding to more than three times the global population, and approximately 3.6 devices per person. Each of these devices represents a valuable data generation source, collecting information about daily interactions between individuals, devices and digital services.

In this context, Machine Learning (ML) emerges as an incredibly powerful technology to automatically extract patterns from enormous amount of data. In general, access to more data allows to capture richer statistical regularities, improve generalization, and adapt to a wider range of users, environments and tasks~\cite{banko-brill-2001-scaling, shalev2014understanding, hestness2017deeplearningscalingpredictable, kaplan2020scalinglawsneurallanguage}. 

However, having access to more data does not necessarily imply that these data can be freely collected or used. In many domains, data are subject to legal, ethical, technical, and social constraints~\cite{eu_gdpr_2016, hiroshima_process_guiding_principles_2023, eu_ai_act_2024, Buick_copyright, eu_ehds_2025, california_ccpa_cpra}. Medical records are protected because they describe a person’s health status. Financial data may reveal income, consumption patterns, debt, or economic vulnerability. Text, images, and user-generated content may be protected by copyright or by terms of service. Location traces may disclose where a person lives, works, studies, or receives medical care. Even apparently harmless digital traces can become sensitive when aggregated over time or combined with other sources of information \cite{netflix_attack}. Data are therefore not only an input to optimization procedures but are also representations of individuals, their habits, preferences, behaviors, and social relations. 

This observation raises a broader question about control. If data generated by personal devices can reveal intimate aspects of people’s lives, who has access to such data, and under which conditions? In practice, large private companies often occupy a privileged position in the digital ecosystem. They design the devices, operating systems, applications, cloud services, and platforms through which data are generated, stored, and processed. As a consequence, they may acquire detailed knowledge about users’ behavior, preferences, movements, purchases, communications, and interactions. This knowledge can be used in many useful ways to improve people’s daily living, but at the same time, it can also be used for profiling, targeting, ranking, persuasion, facial recognition, or other forms of automated decision-making that are not always transparent to the individuals concerned and are beneficial only for few peoples \cite{pasquale_black_box_society_2015, veliz_privacy_is_power_2021}.

The question is therefore not only whether data are useful, but whether the entities that collect and process them can be trusted. Recent enforcement actions involving major technology companies suggest that privacy risks often arise not only from external attacks or accidental data breaches, but also from ordinary platform practices.

The Cambridge Analytica scandal showed how personal information collected through a digital platform could be repurposed for political profiling and targeted persuasion, raising public concern about consent, transparency, and the secondary use of personal data \cite{ico_democracy_disrupted_2018, ico_data_analytics_political_campaigns_2018}. This episode is part of a broader pattern of documented privacy controversies involving major technology companies \cite{cnil_google_gdpr_2019, ftc_youtube_coppa_2019, ftc_twitter_security_data_ads_2022, ftc_amazon_alexa_coppa_2023, texas_ag_google_privacy_2025}. These cases suggest that privacy risks do not arise only from external breaches, but also from the ordinary ways in which platform operators collect, retain, combine, and repurpose personal data.

Similar concerns arise in machine learning, where the demand for large and diverse training datasets creates strong incentives to collect and reuse data at scale. Controversies involving biometric systems trained on scraped or improperly obtained facial images \citeweb{noyb_clearview_criminal_complaint_2025}, copyrighted material used for model training \citeweb{reisner_ai_pirated_books_problem_2025}, and wearable devices capturing data from both users and bystanders \citeweb{svd_meta_ai_glasses_privacy_2026} illustrate how the search for training data can blur the boundary between legitimate data use and unauthorized appropriation.


These concerns highlight two obstacles to the conventional centralized learning paradigm. The first is practical. Data are increasingly generated across large populations of devices, so collecting them into a single repository may require substantial communication and storage costs, making centralized collection inefficient and difficult to maintain at scale. 

The second obstacle concerns privacy and trust. Even when centralization is technically feasible, transferring raw records to a server increases the exposure of sensitive information and requires users or institutions to trust the entity that collects, stores, and processes the data. The problem is therefore not only how to learn from more data, but how to learn from distributed data without requiring all raw records to be pooled under the control of a single actor.

\section{Federated Learning}

Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a response to this challenge \cite{konecny2015federatedoptimizationdistributedoptimizationdatacenter, pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. Instead of collecting raw data in a central repository, FL moves the training procedure closer to the data sources. Clients keep their datasets locally, compute model updates on their own devices, and communicate only these updates to a central server, whose role is to coordinate the training process and aggregate the received information. In the federated setting, the information exchanged with the server is limited, at least by design, to the updates required for collaborative model training.

This design addresses the two obstacles discussed above. First, it reduces the practical burden of centralized data collection: transmitting model updates can be substantially more efficient than transferring large volumes of raw data to a central storage infrastructure. Second, by avoiding the direct transmission of raw records, FL is naturally aligned with the principle of \textit{data minimization}, according to which only data that are directly relevant and necessary to accomplish a specified purpose should be collected \cite{eu_gdpr_2016, eu_regulation_2018_1725, TRUONG2021102402}.

This second property has made FL particularly attractive in privacy-sensitive domains, including healthcare and finance \cite{Rieke2020, ZHANG2021106775, 10.1145/3298981}. In such settings, FL appears to support the idea of \textit{privacy by design}: privacy considerations are incorporated into the learning architecture itself, rather than being treated only as an external constraint. This intuition is also reflected in the original formulation of FL, where the authors observe that the updates exchanged during training ``will generally contain much less information than the raw training data'' \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

We now formalize the federated learning setting, following the original formulation of \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. Let $\mathcal{U}=\{1,\dots,U\}$ denote a finite set of participants, commonly referred to as clients, users, or devices, coordinated by a central server. The objective is to train a single global machine learning model with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}\in\mathbb{R}^d$. Each client $u\in\mathcal{U}$ holds a local dataset $\mathcal{D}_u=\{(\mathbf{x}_i^u, y_i^u)\}_{i=1}^{N_u}$, where $N_u = |\mathcal{D}_u|$ denotes the number of samples available at client $u$. Local datasets may differ both in size and in their underlying data distributions, reflecting the statistical heterogeneity that is typical of federated learning scenarios.

The goal of federated learning is to minimize a global empirical risk obtained by aggregating the local objectives of the clients:

\begin{equation}
\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^d}
\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})
=
\sum_{u=1}^U p_u \mathcal{L}_u(\boldsymbol{\theta}),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_u(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ denotes the local objective of client $u$ evaluated on its local dataset $\mathcal{D}_u$, and $p_u$ is the aggregation weight associated with client $u$. A common choice is $p_u = 1/U$ for all $u$, which assigns the same weight to each client. Alternatively, one may set $p_u = \frac{|\mathcal{D}_u|}{|\mathcal{D}|}$, where
$\mathcal{D} = \bigcup_{u=1}^U \mathcal{D}_u$, so that clients are weighted proportionally to the size of their local datasets.

The local objective is typically written as an Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) problem:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_u(\boldsymbol{\theta})
=
\frac{1}{N_u}
\sum_{i=1}^{N_u}
\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta};\mathbf{x}_i^u,y_i^u),
\end{equation}
where $\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta};\mathbf{x}_i^u,y_i^u)$ is the loss incurred by the model with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ on the $i$-th sample of client $u$.

The original algorithm proposed to solve this optimization problem is Federated Averaging (FedAvg) \cite[Algorithm~1]{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}, an iterative procedure performed over $T$ communication rounds. Its main steps are summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:fedavg}. As discussed above, the training process is coordinated by a central server, which initializes the global model $\model$ (line 1). At each communication round $t$, the server selects a subset of participating clients, denoted by $\mathcal{U}^t \subseteq \mathcal{U}$ (line 3), and sends them the current global model (line 4). After receiving the model, each selected client performs local training on its private dataset (line 6), typically by running stochastic gradient descent for a fixed number of local epochs $E$. Once local training is completed, each client sends back an updated model $\model_u^{t+1} (line 7)$. The server then aggregates the received client models to obtain the next global model $\model^{t+1}$ (line 8).

FedAvg represents the canonical algorithmic formulation of federated learning. Since its introduction, many variants have been proposed, modifying different components of the protocol, including the client optimizer, the server aggregation rule, the client sampling strategy, and the communication pattern \cite{reddi2021adaptivefederatedoptimization}. This line of work shows that federated learning should not be understood as a single fixed algorithm, but rather as a flexible framework for distributed optimization under data locality constraints. At the same time, this flexibility is needed because the federated setting departs from the assumptions of conventional centralized training. Clients may hold data drawn from different distributions \cite{Li2020On, marfoq2021federated, wang_client, scaffold}, operate under different computational \cite{li2019fedmdheterogenousfederatedlearning,FjORD} and network conditions \cite{caldas2019expandingreachfederatedlearning, chen_efficient, ALMANIFI2023100742}, participate intermittently \cite{ribeiro_client_corr, rodio_clients, sun_participation}, and have different privacy requirements \cite{brendan2018learning, Fu_dp_dl_review}. As a result, federated learning introduces a set of challenges that are either absent or less pronounced in centralized learning, including statistical and system heterogeneity \cite{ye_review_het_fl}, communication efficiency \cite{ALMANIFI2023100742}, fairness \cite{Vucinich_fairness, Shi_fairness}, personalization \cite{tan_personalization}, robustness \cite{Uddin_robustness}, and privacy \cite{fu_privacy}.

\begin{algorithm2e}[ht]
\caption[FedAvg: Federated Averaging]{FedAvg: Federated Averaging}
\label{alg:fedavg}
\KwInput{clients $\mathcal{U}$ with local datasets $\{\mathcal{D}_u\}_{u=1}^{U}$; communication rounds $T$; local epochs $E$; mini-batch size $B$; learning rate $\eta$}
\KwOutput{final global model $\boldsymbol{\theta}^T$}
\textbf{server} initializes $\boldsymbol{\theta}^1$\;

\For{$t = 1,\dots,T$}{
    \textbf{server} selects $\mathcal{U}^t \subseteq \mathcal{U}$ clients\;
    \textbf{server} broadcasts $\boldsymbol{\theta}^t$ to the clients in $\mathcal{U}^t$\;
    \ForEach{$u \in \mathcal{U}^t$ \textbf{in parallel}}{
        $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{t+1}_{u}
        \leftarrow
        \operatorname{ClientUpdate}(u,\boldsymbol{\theta}_t,E,B,\eta)$\;
        \textbf{client} sends $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{t+1}_{u}$ to the \textbf{server}\;
    }
    \textbf{server} aggregates $\displaystyle
    \boldsymbol{\theta}^{t+1}
    \leftarrow
    \sum_{u \in \mathcal{U}^t}
    \frac{N_u}{\sum_{v \in \mathcal{U}^t} N_v}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}^{t+1}_{u}$\;
}
\BlankLine
\Procedure{$\operatorname{ClientUpdate}(u,\boldsymbol{\theta},E,B,\eta)$}{
    \textbf{client} splits $\mathcal{D}_u$ into mini-batches $\mathcal{B}_u$ of size $B$\;
    \For{$e = 1,\dots,E$}{
        \ForEach{mini-batch $b \in \mathcal{B}_u$}{
            \textbf{client} updates $\displaystyle
            \boldsymbol{\theta}
            \leftarrow
            \boldsymbol{\theta}
            -
            \eta
            \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}
            \left(
            \frac{1}{|b|}
            \sum_{(\mathbf{x},y)\in b}
            \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta};\mathbf{x},y)
            \right)$\;
        }
    }
    \Return{$\boldsymbol{\theta}$}\;
}
\end{algorithm2e}

\paragraph{Heterogeneity}
Unlike centralized training, where data are typically collected in a single infrastructure and processed under relatively uniform computational conditions, federated learning operates over a population of clients that contribute both data and computation to the training process. This creates two closely related forms of heterogeneity: \textit{statistical heterogeneity}, which concerns differences in the local data distributions, and \textit{system heterogeneity}, which concerns differences in the computational and communication capabilities of the clients.


System heterogeneity arises from differences in clients' hardware, energy availability, memory, bandwidth, and availability over time. These factors determine whether a client can participate in a given communication round, how much local computation it can perform, and how quickly it can return its update to the server. Since such conditions are often outside the direct control of the server, client participation in federated learning is typically partial, intermittent, and uneven across the population.

This uneven participation can affect the training process in several ways. If some clients are more likely to participate than others, the aggregated updates may overrepresent specific regions of the client population, potentially introducing bias in the global model \cite{wang_client}. Moreover, temporal or spatial correlations in client availability can affect convergence and increase the variability of model performance across rounds. System heterogeneity also exacerbates the \textit{straggler effect}, where slower clients delay the completion of a training round or must be dropped from aggregation \cite{kairouz_open_problems}.


Statistical heterogeneity arises because each client generates data through its own local interactions, environment, behavior, and preferences. As a result, the local datasets may differ in size, feature distribution, label distribution, or more generally in the relationship between inputs and outputs \cite{kairouz_open_problems, ye_review_het_fl}. Consequently, the data available at each client may not be representative of the overall population, and the local objectives optimized by different clients may point toward different or even conflicting solutions\cite{scaffold}. Training a single global model under these conditions can therefore lead to a model that performs unevenly across clients, or poorly represents clients whose data differ substantially from the majority \citeA{marfoq2021federated}. 

\paragraph{Personalization}
Closely related to statistical heterogeneity, personalization in federated learning addresses the fact that a single global model may not be equally suitable for all clients \cite{marfoq2021federated}. When clients have different data distributions, the model that performs best on average across the population may perform poorly for specific clients or subgroups. One possible solution would be for each client to train an entirely local model, without participating in the federated process. However, in many cases, individual clients do not have enough data to learn accurate and robust models in isolation. Conversely, participating in standard FL allows clients to benefit from the data indirectly contributed by others, but may produce a global model that fails to capture their local distribution.

Personalized federated learning aims to address this trade-off. The goal is to exploit shared information across clients while still adapting the learned model to each client's local data and task. Several approaches have been proposed to achieve this balance. Some methods learn a common global representation and personalize only part of the model, such as the final layers or client-specific heads \cite{fedper, fedrep}. Others formulate personalization as a regularized optimization problem, where each client learns a local model constrained to remain close to a shared global model \cite{pfedme, ditto}. A further line of work uses clustering or mixture-based approaches, in which clients are grouped according to similarities in their data distributions or learned models, and each group is assigned a more specialized model \cite{marfoq2021federated, marfoq_memorization, fedGMM}
\paragraph{Fairness}
Personalization can improve local utility by adapting models to client-specific objectives and data distributions. Nevertheless, federated training should also ensure that this adaptation does not lead to systematically poor performance for under-represented clients, populations, or protected groups.
Fairness is therefore a central challenge in FL, and different deployments may require different fairness guarantees. Accordingly, several notions of fairness have been considered in the literature \cite{Vucinich_fairness,Shi_fairness, salazar_fairness}. For instance, one may require the final model to satisfy group fairness \cite{salazar_fairness}, similarly to centralized machine learning \cite{dwork_fairness, zemel2013learning_fairness}, by avoiding systematic disparities across protected groups defined by attributes such as race, gender, or age. Beyond group fairness, FL also introduces notions of fairness that are specific to the federated setting. A first one is client-level fairness \cite{li2020fairresourceallocationfederated, erraji2026lossgapparityfairness}, which requires the global model to provide comparable utility across clients, so that no client systematically benefits at the expense of others. A second one concerns participation in the training process. Since only a subset of clients is typically selected at each communication round, frequently selected clients may have a stronger influence on the learned model, whereas rarely selected clients may remain poorly represented \cite{huang_fairness, fairness_shi_client_selection}. Finally, fairness may also be understood in terms of contribution: clients that provide more useful updates, data, or computational resources may need to be compensated or incentivized proportionally to their role in the training process \cite{shi_fairness_incentives, Chen_fairness_incentives}. These different fairness notions raise distinct challenges and can interact with privacy in non-trivial ways \cite{chen_privacy_fairness, fairness_fl_rafi}. In particular, enforcing or auditing group fairness often requires access to sensitive attributes or group-level statistics, which may conflict with the privacy-preserving motivation of FL. As discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:aia}, sensitive attributes used to define fairness groups may themselves be vulnerable to inference attacks during FL training, and different distribution may impact on the vulnearability of the attribute.
